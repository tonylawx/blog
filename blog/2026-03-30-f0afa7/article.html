To be frank, when a man decides to enter marriage today, he actually faces greater risk and uncertainty than a woman does. The most frustrating part is that society offers almost no calm, rational space for an honest conversation about the real pressures borne by the party who appears advantaged on the surface. If men and women cannot sit down and peacefully exchange their respective pains and genuine needs, true balance becomes very hard to reach.

In today's traffic-driven environment, built on outrage and conflict, men's emotional, physical, and retirement needs are routinely mocked and dissected for content. People repeatedly emphasize that men gain reproductive value, household value, sexual value, and emotional value from marriage. It sounds reasonable in the abstract, but in the concrete reality of an individual life, these returns often fail to materialize.

I have known quite a few men who poured enormous sums of money, time, and energy into a marriage. After the divorce, the children almost always stayed with the ex-wife, who frequently badmouthed the ex-husband and his family in front of the kids. Raised solely by the mother, the children naturally grew closer to her and increasingly distant from their father. For these men, the entire marriage yielded almost nothing, and their later years are likely to be lonely and bleak.

The ex-wife, meanwhile, enjoyed stable housing and financial support during the marriage. In the relatively lighter years of child-rearing, she had time to recover her health, cultivate hobbies, and even start a business or pursue a career. The stability of that rearguard was made possible largely because the man was out front bearing responsibility and providing security.

Yet when public discourse tells the story of a woman wronged in marriage who later reinvented herself through entrepreneurship, left her "useless" husband, and pushed back against her in-laws, it instantly becomes the most viral "female empowerment" fantasy. In that narrative, can you really claim the man derived "reproductive value" from the marriage? Hardly. He merely completed the act of reproduction, leaving descendants for his family and for society. And the single most important reason the ex-wife's life didn't completely collapse is that she didn't simply bear children — she devoted herself to raising them to be sunny and healthy, never sowing division in their presence. She would thoughtfully mention the father's strengths and sacrifices, so the children kept some warmth and gratitude toward him. That way, the man at least knows his children will care about him in the future. This — the "raising" half of reproductive value — is the most critical and most easily overlooked piece.

Household value works much the same way. A lot of domestic labor today can be absorbed by a few tens of thousands of yuan worth of appliances. When I moved into my own place, the very first things I bought were a dishwasher and a robot vacuum — precisely to reduce the friction at home. Yet even so, when the topic of sharing chores comes up, these efforts are routinely ignored, and the verdict still lands as "you never do any housework."

Sexual value cannot really be separated from emotional value. Where there is genuine love, both partners attract and satisfy each other, and intimacy is a shared pleasure. Where there is no love, the other person is merely a tool — and there is no real "value" to speak of. Moreover, if you just lie there passively, it's genuinely unclear who is providing value to whom.

The most undervalued — and perhaps the most central — value in modern marriage, in my view, is emotional value.

Not long ago, a trivial incident along the lines of "do you even get to have a cola?" deeply moved many men. After talking with several of them, I realized the emotional desert has become that severe. Back when people were dating, how rare it is for someone to genuinely ask: Was work exhausting today? How's your mood? Is there anything you're craving? How did you sleep? Let me see what you're short on — I'll get it for you. Things are tight this month, but I'll still find a way to surprise you, even if it's just bringing back a piece of candy, or photographing a beautiful cloud or flower. These most basic forms of warmth — it seems many people no longer know how to express them.

If even these fundamental emotional connections are absent, what exactly are you grinding for when you bustle to buy a house, pay the mortgage, hand over the bride price, and toil to support a family? Can you even call that your home?

The most essential meaning of marriage is having, in this world, a partner who truly belongs to you. Someone you can share your daily life with, whose emotions are understood and accepted, whose needs are gently met — mutual encouragement, shared growth. You know they are your anchor, your armor, and your soft spot. With that inner peace and security, you can go out and fight with far more strength.

Personally, I think this is actually harder for men. A man with decent material conditions and reasonable generosity can usually find a partner without too much difficulty. But the investments he makes in marriage are concentrated in the first half of his life — buying a house, earning money, shouldering family responsibility — while the real returns often only become visible in the second half. Whether that partner will still walk beside you when you're old and frail is full of variables. Without a deep emotional bond between you, once your health declines and she is younger than you, a serious illness or a single accident can thrust you into a position of weakness. Public opinion will almost invariably side with the woman, and the children will most likely lean toward their mother. The man's material contributions are repeatedly invoked, while his emotional fragility is routinely mocked and deconstructed.

For a man, who his wife is as a person — her character and whether she genuinely loves him — matters far more than her external attributes.

The tragedy is that many men's upbringing is missing a critical lesson: how to manage family relationships, how to protect the boundaries of the nuclear family, how to articulate emotional needs, and how to maintain a relative balance of rights and responsibilities within the household.

Before marriage, society primarily weighs a man's economic strength. After marriage, what's truly tested is his capacity to steward a relationship. The moment his economic value dips or his body is no longer strong, he becomes easy to "optimize."

Today's discourse rarely discusses how to build, improve, or repair a marriage. Instead, it packages "decisive divorce" as something chic and cathartic.

A man with genuine emotional intelligence, who knows how to tend a family with care, will have far more certainty about late-life returns from his marriage than someone whose marriage was assembled purely from a house, a car, and a bride price.

Yet the reality remains brutal: plenty of men with ordinary material conditions are shut out of the marriage market entirely. If bride prices were ever strictly regulated, and women came to select partners more on emotional capacity and character, it could actually push even more men out — leaving them with no descendants.

And the men fortunate enough to enter marriage and find a like-minded, devoted partner willing to grow old together — they are a tiny minority of the lucky. Because the current climate of opinion and education allows many women to simultaneously profit from both the "traditional woman" and "independent woman" value systems, freely switching stances depending on the setting, and still drawing support either way.

Many women today are already waking up to these contradictory scripts, and they feel their own confusion about future marital choices. Going forward, courtship and marriage may require both parties to continually prove to each other that they are reliable, high-quality partners.

Still, there is one relatively positive signal: as marriage becomes increasingly unstable, the free market will reawaken. People may get second, third, or even more chances to restructure a family. In a life we only get to live once, society is gradually accepting mid-course adjustments; or, in an increasingly atomized society, looser yet genuine family bonds may take shape. Perhaps that, in its own way, counts as a kind of emancipation — a loosening of the pursuit of individual freedom and happiness.
