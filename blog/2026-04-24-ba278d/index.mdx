## 0. Market Read

Today's defining feature was a pop followed by a forked lower close. All four major indices sold off together. The morning was orderly enough, but around midday a sudden geopolitical headline jolted the tape and the market dumped hard. Then, as reports of Iran walking out of talks and air-raid sirens in Tehran got denied one by one, the indices bounced and the day's damage got trimmed.

Inside the tape, it isn't uniform weakness — capital is still rotating structurally. Tech and consumer discretionary lagged, with TSLA and MSFT dragging on their respective complexes. Software kept getting hit under the weight of ServiceNow and IBM prints, and IGV gave back all the way to around 83.5 in one leg. Semiconductors, by contrast, kept attracting bids — the related ETFs ripped again — a sign the market still prefers the AI hardware and compute chain short-term.

The macro data complicated things further. Initial jobless claims rose from 207K to 214K, and continuing claims climbed to 1.821 million — only modestly above expectations. The real tell is the April flash PMIs, which came in clearly strong: manufacturing PMI up to 54, services up to 51.3, composite up to 52. Growth re-accelerating isn't a bad thing in itself, but if goods, services, and energy prices all lift together, the market has to face down inflation pressure again.

## 1. Risk Watch

The **first** risk category is still geopolitical headlines. Today's intraday dump showed that while the market will bounce, it remains very sensitive to headlines around Iran negotiations, air-raid sirens, the Strait of Hormuz, and crude. As long as the weekend carries uncertainty, short-term money can't fully look past tail risk.

The **second** risk is inflation and rate expectations. A big PMI beat says economic resilience is intact, but price pressure can resurface. If subsequent inflation prints stay hot, the valuation pressure on US equities — especially high-multiple growth — comes back, with software being the most sensitive.

The **third** risk is flows. Month-end pension rebalancing is expected to sell roughly $25 billion of US equities, a flow headwind. CTAs bought more than $30 billion last week, that drops to around $20 billion this week, and next week — assuming modest market moves — CTA buying is expected to nearly go to zero. In other words, for the rally to continue, hedge funds need to keep covering shorts or adding longs. Otherwise, the short-term fuel fades meaningfully.

## 2. Names to Watch

**IGV** remains the core read on software sentiment. Software dropped nearly 6% today, with big constituents broadly lower — PLTR, CRM, INTU, ADBE, APP, ORCL all under pressure. IGV's pullback to around 83.5 still sits in a strong support zone, but the near-breakout edge has been interrupted. First it needs to stop bleeding and base sideways — only then can we watch for it to reorganize the next breakout attempt.

**Semiconductors** continue to outperform software, but the short-term setup is clearly crowded. After the consecutive rips in SOXX and SMH, there's no solid defensive zone in between. The focus now is whether the lows of the high-volume up-days hold. Watch 434 on SOXX and 467–475 on SMH. If those levels break, the money that chased the highs turns into overhead, and a short-term minor top can form. The next nearer support on SMH is around 453.

**INTC's** print looks good on the surface: Q1 adjusted EPS of $0.29, revenue of $13.58 billion (up 7.2% YoY), adjusted gross margin of 41%, operating margin of 12.3%. Q2 revenue guidance of $13.8–$14.8 billion also beat. The problem is the multiple. After the post-close surge, on 2027 EPS of $1.00–$1.20, the forward P/E is already 77–80x. Short-term, this is better traded on sentiment and technicals, with nearest support at 68.8.

**AMD** keeps running on the INTC print and the CPU narrative — it got as high as ~330, closer to the ~350 trim-concentration target. That's not a call against AMD's long-term upside. It's about reading your own book: if you already run high semi concentration, the closer to 350 you get, the more appropriate it is to trim incrementally; if your concentration is low, you can keep holding on the long-term thesis.

**MSFT** was the cleanest long-duration execution today. After the pullback to ~416, the remaining 4% add is filled, total position is now 14%, and no further adds from here. Structurally, post-430 it's still treated as right-side repair — try not to lose 397. A second loss of 397 opens the door to retesting the lows below 375. Upside resistance is heavy at 498–516; the long-term target stays 500+.

## 3. Trading Strategy

Strategically, I'm not defining today as trend destruction — I'm defining it as the first clear divergence after a strong rally. The indices trimming their losses tells you buyers are still around. But the combination of software getting hit, semis overheating, headline whipsaw, and month-end flow headwinds means the short-term tape is not one to chase blindly.

**Software** — watch whether IGV's strong support at ~83.5 holds. Don't rush to call a resumption of strength on the day of a big red candle. Only a subsequent base, sideways action, and renewed expansion on volume confirm that active buyers are back. Otherwise, software remains the weak branch inside a repair rally.

**Semis** — existing holders can keep passively riding, but use SOXX 434 and SMH 467–475 as short-term risk-control references. A break of those high-volume-day lows means the chase money is getting trapped and you can no longer treat the complex as unconditionally strong. **INTC**-style gap-ups after the close are better handled by watching whether the 68.8 line holds for sentiment continuation — not by chasing on a fundamentals multiple.

**Single names:** MSFT's add plan is complete — from here it's mostly hold, no easy trim inside 500, no more adds. AMD — approaching 350, decide on personal concentration whether to take some off the table. TSLA — still grinding inside the existing swing range, no new trade signal for now. NVDA — sideways, keep watching.

**NFLX's** buyback is a modest positive support. The company added roughly $25 billion in new repurchase authorization, which together with the unused $6.8 billion gives roughly $30–$32 billion in total. If executed within a year, that's about a 7%–8% EPS accretion. Technically, though, watch 87 — a break below turns the chart back to bearish and opens a return below 77, where a low-buy opportunity may be reconsidered.
