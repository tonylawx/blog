## 0 Market Assessment

The market remains in a tactical environment of "strong individual names, weak-breadth index signals." Index-level action was lifted collectively by earnings, with all 11 S&P sectors up, the Dow and Russell leading, and industrials standing out on CAT results — but the core driver is not broad beta; it is positioning, diversification, and selective rotation. Strong names continue to attract flows, while weak laggards get dragged behind. Short-term, do not fight this individual-name-driven tape.

Macro data gives AI and semiconductor trades a reason to keep clustering: Q1 GDP grew 2%, below the 2.3% expectation, but AI-related investment contributed most of the growth, while residential fixed investment was a clear drag. March headline PCE came in at +0.7% MoM / +3.5% YoY, with core PCE +0.3% MoM / +3.2% YoY — broadly in line. The market is currently defaulting to the view that Middle East negotiations will make progress and the Hormuz Strait will eventually reopen; as long as this base case is not broken by military escalation, short-term risk appetite will keep revolving around strong individual names.

## 1 Risk Warning

The biggest tail risk remains the Middle East. Brent crude holds above $110; on one hand, the market sees Iran potentially submitting a revised proposal and the US attempting to push a coalition to reopen the Hormuz Strait, while on the other the US is still evaluating potential military action against Iran. If the military option re-escalates next week and negotiation expectations fail, the current chase logic could reverse quickly, with both the index and high-beta names facing a re-pricing.

Flows are also diverging. Retail has shifted from underbuying dips to chasing highs, and sentiment has turned frothy; mutual funds, previously underweight, are also topping up leaders. CTAs no longer provide meaningful buying over the next month — under sideways, up, and down scenarios they lean toward modest selling, with accelerated selling only triggered below the 6739 key level, where potential disposal could reach $40–50 billion. Short-term, ride the trend, but do not misread a tactical churn as a risk-free trend.

## 2 Names to Watch

AAPL delivered a solid print: EPS of $2.01 beat the $1.96 expectation, revenue of $111.18 billion grew 17% YoY, with Greater China revenue of $20.5 billion up 28% YoY the main bright spot. The company added $100 billion in buybacks and raised the quarterly dividend to $0.27 per share. AAPL is not deeply involved in the AI data-center arms race, so its free cash flow pressure is low — it should be viewed as a steady cash-flow and buyback-driven name; but growth is on the flatter side. The Sep-26 valuation range is $170–255, Sep-27 is $186–279, with a median of $233; technically, a decisive break above $281 is needed to truly open space.

LLY continues to show high-quality growth: adjusted EPS of $8.55 clearly beat the $6.97 expectation, revenue grew 56% YoY to $19.8 billion versus the $17.8 billion estimate, full-year adjusted EPS guidance was raised to $35.5–37, revenue guidance raised to $82–85 billion, and the credit rating was upgraded. QCOM's results themselves were not spectacular — EPS of $2.65 and revenue of $10.6 billion only modestly beat — but what really drove the sharp move higher was the CEO's commentary on data-center chips, the XPU/CPU/custom-chip/networking-chip portfolio, shipments beginning in the December quarter, and the view that China handset revenue bottomed in Q3. QCOM has broken $175 and turned technically right-sided, but fundamental delivery still needs subsequent verification.

## 3 Trading Strategy

The strategic core right now is to respect strength but control chasing. For AAPL, a break above $281 can be read as the technical lid lifting after a long consolidation; steadier capital can treat it as a slow-variable allocation supported by buybacks and dividends — absent that break, do not expect fast returns. LLY still has a long-term hold logic in the $700–1000 range, but the closer to $700 the larger the safety cushion; aggressive sizing can be small, while the core position is better suited to waiting for pullbacks or managing cost via trimming into strength and adding on weakness.

QCOM is an expectations trade after a right-side breakout — manage risk around the breakout level and strong support below, but recognize that this rally is driven mostly by management commentary about forward data-center business; the current and next-quarter guidance have not yet reflected the earnings increment, so a fast spike can easily be followed by a retest. AMD has broken $348 and continues to push toward the prior high, but volume is contracting — chase cautiously. MSFT is short-term choppy on post-earnings volatility and OpenAI lawsuit expectations, but the long-term Azure and AI logic remains intact. NVDA is back near $200; the principle remains "don't chase spikes — pullbacks are the better entry."
