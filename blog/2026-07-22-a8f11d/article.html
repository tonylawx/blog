0 · MARKET

· Market Snapshot

All four major indexes dipped on July 22. S&P 500 breadth was roughly even — half up, half down. Philadelphia Semiconductor volume shrank sharply, ETFs included, signaling thin intraday trading with all eyes on after-hours earnings. On the geopolitical front, the U.S. warned it would strike Iranian infrastructure if ships were fired upon in the Strait of Hormuz; Iran countered with threats against allied energy facilities in the Middle East. The Bank of Japan maintained its rate-hike forward guidance and signaled it may increase the pace of tightening — a variable that keeps pressure on Asia-Pacific capital flows.

1 · FOCUS

· TXN: Overvalued but Unscathed

TXN delivered a solid if unspectacular quarter. Q2 EPS came in at $2.14 versus the $1.94 consensus, with revenue of $5.46B beating the $5.26B estimate. Q3 guidance was raised modestly — EPS of $2.23–$2.57, revenue of $5.65–$6.15B, both slightly above the Street's $2.18 and $5.63B. The beat was real but narrow, a few dimes of upside.

Shares pulled back to around $285 after hours, landing on intermediate support without breaching the critical $263 bear trigger. The picture looks like a high-base consolidation: $312 is the breakout level for new highs, while losing $263 opens a mean-reversion path toward $200. TXN passed its earnings test, but the overvaluation overhang persists.

2 · SOFTWARE

· IBM and NOW: Two Fates in Software

IBM beat on Q2 revenue ($17.2B vs. $16.8B expected) and EPS ($2.93), but cut full-year sales guidance to $70.2–$70.9B — the terminal value falling below the $70.8B consensus. Won the quarter, lost the year. Technically, the stock has broken below $235 and then $213. At $205, it sits near the lower bound of its 2027 forward valuation range ($197). Not expensive, but structurally weak — needs to reclaim $257 to re-enter an uptrend, otherwise more basing ahead.

NOW posted textbook-steady results. Q2 revenue of $3.987B grew 24% year-over-year, subscription revenue hit $3.877B (+24.5%), and remaining performance obligations reached $13.2B (+21%). Full-year subscription guidance was raised to $15.76–$15.78B. Under both high-growth and conservatively adjusted forward PE frameworks, the $100–$120 range is the core value zone. After-hours action pushed the stock back up to the $100 resistance wall — the key tell is whether it can hold $102–$103 over the next two sessions. A clean break above $120 unlocks 2027 valuation upside; losing the June 25 low changes the outlook.

The broader software sector was sold again. Google's 2026 capex raise pressured hyperscalers and their software ecosystem, while channeling fresh momentum into semiconductors. Semis and software are running an inverse seesaw — until MSFT, AMZN, and META report next week with their own capex forecasts, the software side stays under pressure.

3 · EARNINGS

· TSLA: Revenue Growth Can't Hide the Margin Collapse

TSLA dropped over 4% after hours on a quarter short on bright spots. Revenue of $28.2B grew 26% year-over-year, but adjusted EPS of $0.33 badly missed the $0.51 consensus — roughly 65% of expectations. Operating margin cratered to 1.4%. Operating cash flow surged 85% to $4.697B, but capex exploded 142% to $5.789B, pushing quarterly free cash flow to negative $1.092B. The spend went into AI compute, batteries, semiconductors, and humanoid robot production buildout — all hard capex.

Deliveries were a bright spot: 500,100 units in Q2, a record for the period. Inventory days dropped from 27 to 15. FSD subscribers grew 56% to 1.48 million, with North American new-vehicle FSD take rate exceeding 55% for the first time. Robotaxi service expanded in Austin and launched fully driverless operations in Miami, Orlando, and Tampa in July.

But expectations for the humanoid robot need tempering. Musk acknowledged it will be Tesla's hardest product to scale, constrained by battery technology, model maturity, and reliability. Large-scale deployment remains years away.

Technically, shares have slid to the lower bound of the $315–$368 trading range. On a 40–60x forward PE basis, the 2026 ceiling is $181 and 2027 is $158 — a severe disconnect with market price. Swing traders face a $40 downside stop versus $50–$60 of upside to the $419 resistance. If $315 breaks, the next landing zone is the $200s.

4 · CAPEX

· GOOG: Cloud Revenue Explodes, but 2027 Capex Is the Real Cliffhanger

GOOG posted Q2 revenue of $119.8B, up 24% year-over-year and ahead of the $117.0B consensus. Reported EPS of $9.11 looked like a massive beat versus the $2.88 estimate, but roughly $98B of that came from SpaceX equity investment gains — capital gains, not operating income, and not useful as a recurring earnings benchmark. Strip that out and the operating beat was modest. Operating margin was 34%, just below the 34.7% expectation.

Segment results were broadly strong: Google Services revenue $94.54B (+15%), ad revenue $81.63B (+14%), YouTube ads $11.06B (+13%), all beating estimates. The standout was Google Cloud at $24.77B in revenue, up 82% year-over-year, crushing the $22.46B estimate. That number confirms AI monetization through cloud and enterprise channels is accelerating fast.

On capex, Q2 spend was $44.92B — roughly double the year-ago quarter. Full-year 2026 guidance was raised to $195–$205B (from $180–$190B), an uplift of about $15B. What Google will spend in 2026 is now mostly settled. The real suspense is 2027. For the second consecutive quarter, Google said only that "2027 capex will increase significantly" — identical language, zero specifics.

Here lies a massive expectations gap. Buy-side consensus puts 2027 Google capex at $350–$375B, implying 70–80% year-over-year growth. Sell-side terminal estimates sit at just $250B, or 20–30% growth. The spread is $100B, roughly 50 percentage points of growth rate. The lofty forward EPS currently baked into semiconductor valuations is largely anchored to that aggressive buy-side number. If growth lands closer to the sell-side's 20–30%, a significant chunk of semi EPS gets repriced downward.

Capex will grow. The question is the growth rate. Markets trade on expectations — "buy the rumor, sell the fact." If the rate slides from high growth to moderate growth, the elevated EPS multiples built on the high-growth scenario have to be marked down, even as absolute spending keeps rising.

Google's own valuation is not stretched: 2026 forward range $286–$430, 2027 range $297–$460 (midpoint $372), with the stock at $341 sitting comfortably in the middle. Strong support down to $298. But until the 2027 capex number is on the table, the yawning buy-side/sell-side gap keeps the market in limbo. Whether MSFT, AMZN, and META can deliver clearer 2027 capex signals next week will determine the next move in this seesaw.
