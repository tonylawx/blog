## Market Read

On April 20, US equities entered clear "wait-and-see" mode. The weekend flipped the US-Iran situation yet again — Iran re-announced a blockade of the Strait of Hormuz, and the US attacked and seized an Iranian cargo vessel — which rapidly cooled the optimism that Friday's "Strait reopening" had sparked. Oil bounced back from having "one foot in the grave," while US equities came under only mild pressure and volume shrank notably. That tells you the market is in no rush to re-bet: it's waiting on Wednesday's key checkpoint to see whether the situation extends, renegotiates, or escalates directly.

The single most important call right now: short-term, the tape is still trading geopolitical expectations, not a fundamental regime change. If the situation keeps de-escalating between now and Wednesday, indices can still be nudged slightly higher. If the conflict re-escalates, the market will most likely pivot and head back down in a hurry. More importantly, this rally isn't being driven purely by "new long money." There's mounting evidence that a large share of the recent rally has come from CTA buying, macro short covering, hedge funds being forced to unwind hedges, and retail chasing at the margin. The structure of this move isn't especially solid.

In addition, even though the S&P has made new highs, market breadth is clearly weak. The equal-weight index has only just gotten back to its prior high, while the S&P 500 IT and Communications sectors have contributed the lion's share of this rally's gains. The market is still being dragged higher by the mega-caps and a few strong sectors — this is not a healthy, broad-based advance. Translation: new highs in the index do not equal a healthy structure, and they certainly don't mean the market can't trade back lower.

## Risk Watch

**First**, the US-Iran negotiating track ahead of Wednesday remains the biggest risk variable. The parties involved are giving contradictory accounts of the timing, the seniority of participants, the ceasefire terms, and the blockade conditions — a clear sign that the "de-escalation expectation" is fragile. The market is only temporarily trading it in the optimistic direction. If the deal gets pushed out or the conditions fall apart, risk assets face a fast re-pricing.

**Second**, the volume and structure of this rally are unconvincing. Today's volume contracted sharply, which means a lot of money is sitting on its hands rather than chasing price at the highs. In this kind of environment, even if the index holds its level, it feels more "suspended" than "anchored."

**Third**, the short-term flow-driven bid is approaching a phase high. The dominant trade from hedge funds lately has been ETF short covering, not large-scale additions of net longs. Net leverage hasn't returned to extremes, which means there's still room for more covering — but "the easy part of the short squeeze" is already well behind us, and the bar for the next leg higher keeps getting tougher.

**Fourth**, tech and semiconductors are themselves deep into overbought territory. If flows start rotating out of semis and back into software, or if the geopolitical situation flips back and overall risk appetite softens, the high-flying tech complex can lead a technical correction. That correction doesn't necessarily turn the medium-to-long-term view bearish, but short-term volatility will clearly expand.

## Names to Watch

The thing to watch most closely right now isn't any single name — it's the migration of capital among a few key sectors.

**First, software (IGV)** is the most important inflection zone right now. The author emphasizes that if IGV can decisively break above 88.6, it means the software complex has officially shed its prior overhead suppression, which can trigger a larger wave of short covering and momentum-chasing flows. If that break holds, software's "better days" may be coming back.

**Second, semiconductors** need to start guarding against "good news that no longer produces a synchronized rally." Both SOXX and SMH are now in clear overbought territory. If software breaks out and siphons off incremental flows, semis — even without an immediate crash — can easily slip into a sideways or technical pullback repair phase. Strong support for SOXX sits around 348–368; for SMH it's around 409–419. These levels are pullback-watch zones, not trend-reversal levels.

**Third**, the broader market is still betting that the AI leadership trade keeps running. Hedge funds have been shifting from covering macro shorts back into buying tech and communications services names, and the core bet is still artificial intelligence — particularly the hardware supply chain. The AI logic isn't over; short-term, sector rotation will just get more violent.

## Trading Strategy

On the short-term tape, the most important thing right now is not to chase, but to control your rhythm. Before Wednesday, the market will most likely keep swinging on US-Iran headlines, so it's better to treat the index as an "event-driven high-level waiting zone" rather than an unconditional uptrend. Before the news resolves, the risk/reward on adding aggressively is poor.

Structurally, focus on one question: "who picks up the baton, and who gets siphoned off?" If software can't break out, it means capital is still clustered in the mega-cap tech and semis — in which case semis can stay strong at the highs for a while longer. But if IGV clears 88.6 and software becomes the new covering target, semis need to be defended against a technical breather. That's not a bearish call on AI — it's a reminder to respect short-term flow dynamics.

From an intermediate-term perspective, the author remains positive on the tech, semiconductor, and AI chain outlook, because they are still the strongest-earnings, best-cycle pockets in the S&P 500. But if a pullback does show up short-term, it shouldn't be read as the end of the trend — it looks more like a normal rebalancing after an overbought run. Put differently: right now it's better to "watch structure, wait for levels" than to substitute emotion for discipline at the highs.
