0 · MACRO

Macro warnings: geopolitical tension pushes crude oil higher, fueling rate hike fears

Global financial markets have faced intense macro turbulence since Monday. Geopolitical risks spiked as Trump announced plans to reinstate the blockade on Iran and control the Strait of Hormuz, alongside a proposed 20% security transit fee on cargo ships. Brent crude surged 9% back above $80 a barrel, elevating global inflation risks and hitting risk assets. Fed Governor Waller delivered hawkish remarks, warning that a strong core CPI print this week could prompt near-term rate hikes. Rate swap markets quickly repriced, sending the probability of a September rate hike above 70%, with a potential second hike priced for March 2027. This tightening macro environment puts severe valuation pressure on tech stocks, especially highly leveraged AI names.

1 · LIQUIDITY

Leverage collapse: KOSPI crash and forced liquidations warnings

Under the weight of high rates and geopolitical friction, the South Korean stock market experienced a brutal deleveraging phase. The KOSPI plunged nearly 9%, falling below the 7,000 level. This marked its third-largest single-day drop since the collapse of Lehman Brothers. With seven circuit breakers triggered this year, liquidity panic reached extreme levels. Foreign funds and domestic institutions exited in size, with foreign investors dumping $100 billion over the past few months. In contrast, South Korean retail investors leveraged up to buy Samsung and SK Hynix. SK Hynix fell 15.4% in a single day. Over the weekend, banks tightened margin lending limits, draining available liquidity. This triggered a massive wave of forced liquidations on Monday, with 320,000 to 360,000 retail leverage accounts wiped out as their balances hit zero. This leverage collapse is a clear warning for US market speculators chasing semiconductors with high leverage.

2 · FOCUS

AI debt line: massive debt issuance and FCF discount realities

Bubbles are also forming at the micro level, driven by a mismatch between rapid AI debt growth and slower monetization. Year to date, six tech giants, including AMZN, GOOG, NVDA, META, ORCL, and SpaceX, have issued a record $182 billion in investment-grade bonds. This is a 13% increase from the same period in 2025 and accounts for 15% of all US corporate bond issuance. If huge capital expenditures (CapEx) fail to convert into revenue quickly enough to generate positive free cash flow (FCF), these giants will have to issue new debt to pay off old obligations. This dynamic will raise financial leverage, trigger credit downgrades, and trap companies in a cycle of borrowing to pay high interest while squeezing profits, ultimately forcing a marketwide revaluation. Consensus estimates point to 2026 as the cyclical bottom for free cash flow among the top four tech firms. If AI monetization fails, the subsequent valuation write-downs could dwarf the dot-com bust of 2000.

3 · SEMIS

Semiconductor divergence: Nvidia valuation bottom and long-term de-leveraging

To avoid debt defaults and credit collapses, tech giants might execute a sharp reversal in 2027, cutting their capital expenditure budgets to negative growth. Such a move would dismantle current semiconductor earnings forecasts, triggering heavy volatility across the sector. Both SOXX and SMH are undergoing a pullback and a transition in ownership. Deleveraging now is constructive for healthier growth over the long term. Compared to the speculative panic in South Korea, the US semiconductor sector features higher institutional participation. The current pullback represents volatility rather than systemic liquidation. Long-term investors do not need to be overly bearish, as opportunity remains in stocks with strong fundamentals. For instance, NVDA is trading at its lowest forward price-to-earnings ratio since 2019. Even if its growth slows due to its massive 80% market share, it is transitioning from a high growth stock to a stable value play. Conservative investors can look to accumulate during pullbacks.
