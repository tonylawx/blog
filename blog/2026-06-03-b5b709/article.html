US Equities Are No Longer an Index Bull Market — They're an AI Stock-Picking Market

US markets continued higher today. All four major indices gained, with the Russell leading and utilities and energy showing relative strength. On the surface, sentiment looks fine.

But the real story isn't how much the indices rose.

It's that this market looks less and less like an "index tape."

It increasingly resembles an AI stock-picking market.

In other words: indices rising does not mean most stocks are rising. What's driving the market remains a small group of AI, semiconductor, and mega-cap tech names.

0 Market Assessment

The current market is not a broad bull market — it's capital constantly hunting for the next AI winner.

Whoever can tell a fresh AI story gets the flow; whoever's story stalls sees capital rotate out instantly.

So the most important question is not whether the S&P can keep rising. It's: where is capital heading next?

1 Index Distortion

The S&P and Nasdaq look strong lately, but index strength is not market strength.

Over the past few sessions, indices kept printing new highs even as a large number of constituents fell daily. Gains are increasingly concentrated.

This isn't all boats rising — it's a handful of mega-caps dragging the index up.

That's why so many investors feel dissonance: the index looks like a bull market; their own portfolio looks flat.

This is no longer a traditional index market. It has become an AI-led stock-picking market where NVDA, AMD, MRVL, AVGO are the true sentiment drivers.

2 Software and Semis Are Fighting for the Same Money

The most visible shift today: capital flooded back into semiconductors.

MRVL ripped after Jensen Huang called it "possibly the next trillion-dollar company," and kept rising after-hours. That single line reignited semiconductor sentiment.

Inside the Philadelphia Semiconductor Index, 27 of 30 constituents rose. The takeaway: capital isn't leaving AI — it's rotating direction within AI.

Software had been red-hot in prior sessions, with IGV drawing intense inflows. Call volume hit records and retail flows were extreme.

But the moment semis ripped today, software got sold.

That's the defining feature of this market: it's not that there's no money — it's that money rotates fast between AI branches.

Software, semis, cybersecurity, custom silicon — they're all part of the AI trade. But capital won't favor all of them at once. It will go where the narrative is freshest, the elasticity highest, and the speculating easiest.

3 MRVL vs AVGO: Same Logic, Different Phase

MRVL exploded today on the core thesis of custom AI silicon and high-speed connectivity.

The simple version: AI data centers need more than GPUs — they need custom chips, high-speed interconnects, networking silicon, optical communications, and data transport.

That's why capital is repricing MRVL.

But the problem is obvious: the stock has run too far, too fast.

On a long-duration narrative, MRVL absolutely has imagination. But jumping straight to a trillion-dollar market cap requires a very long earnings bridge.

Right now this is mostly capital front-running expectation.

AVGO, by comparison, has more mature fundamentals, a bigger business, and is the leader in custom silicon. But its market cap is already huge, so short-term elasticity is lower than MRVL's.

That's exactly why the tape prefers MRVL: not because it's necessarily better than AVGO, but because it's smaller and easier for flows to push.

4 Software

Software isn't broken, but the rhythm has gotten more complicated.

The key level for IGV remains 101. A break below sends software back into the chop zone.

That doesn't make software bearish — it just means this fast squeeze leg is paused for now.

If IGV holds 101, the swing risk/reward from here is roughly $3 of downside against initial upside to 109–111.

But for software to re-open to the upside, semis need to cool first. Otherwise flows keep rushing semis and software can't rally in parallel.

Cybersecurity is similar. PANW, CRWD, FTNT have stable fundamentals but rich valuations. They're running on crowding logic. As long as flows keep crowding in, they stay strong. The moment high-level positioning loosens, the pullback will be fast.

5 Risk

The biggest risk right now is not an immediate index crash — it's that the market is getting more extreme.

First, capital is increasingly concentrated. Second, the AI leaders can't slip. Third, the profit tape is getting thicker.

When capital concentrates in a few names, that's a short-term positive — the leaders keep rising and the index stays propped. But long-term it makes the market more fragile, because the moment a leader stumbles, the index feels it immediately.

Especially the names that have ripped recently with almost no pullback and no intermediate support — those profit tapes are very thick. Once that money wants out, it moves fast.

6 SpaceX Could Be the Rotation Trigger

The next datapoint to watch is the SpaceX listing.

If SpaceX actually starts trading, it could siphon off a chunk of speculative capital — capital that's currently in semis, AI software, high-valuation growth, and cybersecurity. Especially the names that have ripped over the past two months and are sitting on big unrealized gains.

When a fresh super-hot theme emerges, some capital will choose to cash out of the old theme and chase the new one.

This won't necessarily top the market immediately, but it could act as a redistribution trigger.

So over the next one to two weeks, watch carefully whether high-level AI names show signs of profit-taking.

7 Trading Strategy

This is not a tape to blindly chase just because indices print new highs.

The better playbook: keep the strongest mainline exposure, wait for pullbacks, control your chasing, and focus where capital is actually concentrating.

Semis remain the strongest mainline. Software and cybersecurity still have a chance, but only if flows rotate back.

MRVL-type explosive runners aren't suited to emotional chasing. AVGO's thesis is more solid but less elastic. Mega-cap AI cores like MSFT remain worth watching on pullbacks to key support.

Whether IGV holds 101 is the most important signal for whether the software trade continues.

8 Conclusion

This US rally isn't over yet.

But the market has entered a more extreme phase.

Indices rising, capital concentrating, AI leaders sucking the air out of the room — and risk accumulating.

So don't just watch the S&P and Nasdaq.

What really matters is whether capital keeps chasing the AI leaders.

As long as the AI leaders hold up, the indices can be propped.

But if those leaders start wobbling, the market will be far more fragile than the index suggests.

This isn't an index bull market anymore — it's an AI stock-picking market.
