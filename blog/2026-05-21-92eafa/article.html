0 Market Assessment

All four majors closed sharply higher, and today wasn't just a few mega-caps dragging the index. Semis, software, rate-sensitives, and high-momentum growth all rallied together, with the Russell 2000 clearly leading. News that Middle East negotiations were entering a final stage pulled oil lower — Brent retreated from the 110 area to the mid-105s — and inflation expectations and long-end yield pressure eased in tandem, giving both stocks and bonds room to breathe.

The quality of this bounce is better than the past few sessions, but it can't yet be read as risk-off. The minutes from the April FOMC meeting were clearly hawkish, with rising internal support for removing dovish language and tightening further if inflation stays above 2%. Next quarter's meeting under new Chair Warsh becomes the key checkpoint for the policy framework. Short term, the market is being repaired by geopolitical de-escalation; medium term, the path still depends on whether rates, inflation, and policy language all cooperate.

1 Risk Watch

The biggest risk is in crowded trades. Semiconductors are now one of the most crowded exposures globally — retail capital concentrated in hot chip names, call buying, and leveraged tools like SOXL, while hedge fund short positions have risen to a 10-year high and gross leverage sits at elevated levels. This structure rhymes with 2021: short-term short squeezes can keep going, but the further it runs, the worse the risk-reward for late-stage chasers.

The other risk is "good earnings, stock still sells off." INTU printed beats on quarterly EPS, revenue, and full-year guidance, yet still gapped down hard after-hours and broke prior lows — a sign software hasn't yet regained strong enough capital consensus. AI tooling can absolutely boost efficiency, but it has yet to drive revenue acceleration; if the market keeps rewarding only hardware and speculative beta, undervalued software names can keep grinding along the bottom even with decent fundamentals.

2 Names in Focus

NVDA's earnings are unambiguously strong: Q1 revenue of $81.62 billion versus $79.19 billion expected; adjusted EPS of $1.87 versus $1.76 expected; data center revenue of $75.2 billion, up 21% sequentially and 92% YoY. Next-quarter revenue guidance around $91 billion, slightly ahead of expectations; gross margin still around 75%; and the board raised the buyback authorization to $80 billion. Fundamentals remain strong, but the market cap is so large that the healthiest path is not a vertical rip — it's a sideways repair that drifts gradually higher.

INTU's core tension is that cheap valuation and capital aversion coexist. Forward valuation bands for July 2026 and July 2027 sit materially above the current price, and analyst targets remain high — the fundamental pricing isn't bad. But growth slowed from 15% YoY last year to around 10%, and AI integration has yet to visibly accelerate revenue, so the market first marks it down as "no growth surprise." Large IPOs like SPCX and OpenAI are worth tracking, but until full deal size, price range, and financial details are clear, they aren't suitable for short-term narrative-driven trades.

3 Trading Strategy

Trade with cautious optimism — don't chase the last leg of the crowded semi trade. Existing semi exposure can let the trend do the talking, but take-profit and stop-loss levels must be written down in advance, especially when using leveraged ETFs or short-dated options. Don't mistake a short-term squeeze for long-term allocation logic. NVDA — a pullback below 211, or even toward 200, is actually a better level to re-evaluate adding risk; churning sideways around 220 is not the place to lean in with heavy size chasing post-earnings sentiment.

Software is best handled in layers. MSFT and IGV are more trend-repair setups — watch whether they can form sustained capital inflows. INTU, as a beaten-down cheap name post-earnings, suits only those who can tolerate longer waits and bigger swings; below 300 is a strong-support watch zone, but "cheap" alone doesn't override the fact that sector capital preference hasn't yet rotated. At the index level, as long as oil and long-end yields keep pulling back, the rally can extend; the moment yields reverse higher or Middle East de-escalation unravels, exposure must immediately shift back to defensive.
