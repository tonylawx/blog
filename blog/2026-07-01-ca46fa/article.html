0 · Market — Index Up Doesn't Mean Your Book Is Making Money

On the last trading day of June, all four major indices closed higher on the surface, but the underlying breadth was poor. Of SPX constituents, 213 advanced and 290 declined. Only tech and industrials posted meaningful gains among the 11 sectors; consumer discretionary and basic materials finished slightly green, and the remaining seven sectors fell, three by more than 1%. This is not a broad return of risk appetite — it's a handful of heavyweights holding up the index.

This kind of structure is unfriendly to diversified books. When the index prints new highs or sits near them, your portfolio doesn't necessarily follow, because capital is concentrated in a small number of large-cap names and a small number of themes. Month-end also brought pension-rebalance selling, so some of the sectors that led in June were sold on the closing day — short-term price signals are distorted to a degree.

The macro data didn't offer a particularly dovish read either. May job openings ticked down from 7.618M to 7.594M, above the 7.30M expected, with the openings rate holding at 4.6%. But consumer confidence fell from 93.1 to 91.2, missing the 94.8 estimate; the share of respondents saying jobs are hard to find rose to 22.5%, the highest since January 2021. The labor market hasn't broken, but the lived experience is deteriorating — compounded by AI's overhang on job prospects, consumer confidence remains soft.

1 · Structure — June's Real Leaders Weren't Tech

June's sector performance defies intuition. Six of the 11 S&P sectors rose and five fell. The top gainer was industrials, up 7.25% on the month, followed by healthcare at +6.62%. The worst laggard was communication services, down 7.16%, with energy off 4.97%. The pressure on communication services is closely tied to heavyweight names like META.

Tech, perhaps counterintuitively, was not the real story in June. Some sub-themes within tech were lively and a few names ran hard, but the broad tech sector essentially flatlined for the month — down about 0.14%. After two consecutive months of strong gains in April and May, June was mostly internal divergence and rotation rather than broad-based expansion.

That means the first week of the second half should focus on where capital re-deploys, not just on index levels. The past few sessions were distorted by month-end repositioning and sector direction was noisy. Once fresh monthly capital begins to settle, the relative strength among industrials, healthcare, tech and consumer becomes much more informative.

2 · Memory — The Defensive Logic in Consumer Stocks Is Breaking Down

Consumer stocks can no longer be treated reflexively as defensive assets. In June, the consumer-staples sector rose about 0.89% while consumer discretionary fell about 2.78% — neither is strong; staples simply held up better. High inflation expectations remain the core pressure: oil has come down from the futures side, but the cost pass-through has already happened, and pump prices and retail goods typically lag the upstream decline.

The problem with consumer discretionary is weaker pricing power. Dining out, athletic apparel and experiential spending are all categories where consumers can cut frequency. Once companies push prices too far, shoppers simply reduce visit count, and both results and confidence take a hit. Staples have stronger must-buy characteristics — groceries, food and daily necessities can absorb some cost pass-through, but that only means "more defensive," not that valuations can ignore the cycle.

So consumer names are not the "buy when the index is too high" substitute they used to be. What matters now is comparing pricing power, valuation elasticity and whether the technicals have actually stabilized — not just screening by whether the ticker has the word "consumer" in its sector label.

3 · Semi — MCD, NKE, COST and WMT Are Diverging Clearly

MCD sits in consumer discretionary. Its valuation zone is approaching "cheap," but growth has decelerated from low-double-digits to high-single-digits, so the forward PE needs a discount. Technically, the breaks below 281 and 269 have built overhead resistance; to confirm a real recovery, the key is whether it can reclaim 286. To the downside, 260, 267, 239–256 and 223–228 are reference zones, but the current action looks more like searching for a bottom than the start of a right-side trend.

NKE's issues are more direct. The latest quarter delivered EPS of $0.20 on revenue of $10.97B, down 1.1% year-over-year — beating the $0.12 and $10.85B consensus on the surface, but the line items are not good: North America fell on a constant-currency basis, China/Europe/Middle East/Africa remained soft, and Converse revenue declined sharply. To stabilize technically it needs to reclaim 47.5 first; 38.4–41.8 below is only a short-term buffer, and the validity of longer-term support should not be overestimated. If the earnings malaise drags on, there's also tail risk from index reconstitution.

COST and WMT sit in staples, with more resilient business models but a shared problem: expensive valuations. COST's membership-fee pricing power is a genuine edge that has historically justified a premium multiple — but that doesn't mean there's much upside left. 867 is an important line; a break below it opens the door to mean reversion and potentially revisits sub-800. WMT's digital transition and pricing power are decent, but around 113 it remains rich versus forward earnings; 104 down to 92.8 is the more critical potential stabilization zone, and if 92.8 gives way, a correction lasting more than a year becomes possible.

4 · Events — Trading Strategy: Cash Is a Position Too

You can watch consumer stocks, but don't chase them. Discretionary needs both earnings and technicals to repair; staples need valuation and margin of safety to reappear. Treating consumer names as a hedge right now still means taking equity risk, and many of these names are not cheap.

If the only goal is to reduce portfolio volatility, cash, money-market funds, short-term Treasuries or a brokerage cash sweep are cleaner tools. The rate path is still unclear, cash yields are not low, and holding dry powder preserves the optionality to buy the next dip and add on conviction. The most important thing at the start of the second half isn't chasing some consumer name that looks cheap — it's watching where capital chooses to go once the month-end noise clears.
