## 0 The Storm Begins

Today's US tape was no ordinary pullback. All four major indices sold off in sync — the Nasdaq down nearly 5%, tech down about 6%, and the Philadelphia Semiconductor Index down 10% in a single session. Of the 30 SOX constituents, almost all were buried. **MRVL**, Micron, and **ARM** — the biggest prior gainers — were hit hardest, while **ASML**, TSMC, and **NVDA** (the heavyweight names) saw relatively contained declines.

It's tempting to stare at one red candle, but what matters isn't "how much it fell." What matters is that this candle was driven by three forces hitting simultaneously: rate expectations reawakening, AI mega-caps confronting financing pressure, and the SpaceX IPO pulling attention away from high-volatility money. With those three together, the most crowded trade — semiconductors — was always going to get hit first.

## 1 Payoffs Re-ignited Rates

May nonfarm payrolls came in at +172k, well above the 85k expectation, and the prior two months were revised up a combined 93k. The unemployment rate held at 4.3%, labor force participation was stable, and leisure/hospitality job growth was especially strong. This print may not change the outcome of the June meeting itself, but it does change the market's imagination about the path of rates beyond.

Rate expectations shifted from "maybe no more hikes" to "maybe one more hike before year-end." For growth stocks, that's no small thing. When long-end rates rise again, the discount rate in DCF models compresses the value of distant cash flows — and the assets that depend most on future growth and high-valuation narratives get repriced first. Semiconductors and the AI chain sit squarely at the most sensitive point of that pressure.

## 2 AI Enters the Cash-Burn Verification Phase

**Meta** is reportedly looking to raise tens of billions of dollars via a stock offering to keep funding AI; **Google** had previously launched an ~$85B equity raise. This signal is more important than any single stock's daily move. In the past, when Big Tech needed cash, they leaned on debt issuance and free cash flow — rarely on massive equity issuance. The new question: the AI arms race isn't just a technology story, it's a cash flow story.

If AI spending delivers revenue acceleration and a rebound in free cash flow, it's an industrial revolution. If Big Tech burns the cash without validating the high-spend-high-return thesis, then "AI revolution" becomes "AI ruin." Microsoft is relatively more deserving of a core-watch slot because it's closest to AI monetization validation and its free cash flow recovery path is clearer. But that entire premise still rests on AI succeeding. The moment that premise wobbles, tech, semis, software, and AI apps are all in the same boat.

## 3 Hot Money Switched Tables

With the SpaceX IPO approaching, many brokers have opened indications of interest. The $135 pricing isn't cheap on fundamentals, but short-term speculators don't care about valuation — they care about scarce early float and potentially extreme volatility. Stocks that have ripped over the past two months with thick profit tapes and crowded speculative positioning become easy ATMs.

That's why today's biggest losers were mostly the biggest prior winners. Hot money's logic is simple: you've already booked profits in semis, memory, and space — take a slice and chase the next high-volatility window. The problem is that when everyone thinks the same way at once, the formerly hot trades suddenly lose their bid. Highly levered participants are especially exposed — profits not taken and principal re-deployed means one pullback can wipe out an entire winning streak.

## 4 Semis Haven't Fully Broken, But the Alarm Has Sounded

The Philadelphia Semiconductor Index landed exactly on its steep uptrend line from April — trend destruction cannot yet be declared. **SOXX** is similar: it has reached the 489–532 support zone but hasn't officially confirmed a breakdown. The real tell is next week's open: a continuation gap below the trendline accelerates selling pressure meaningfully; a higher-open-and-reclaim keeps this in "violent pullback" territory for now.

**SMH**'s pattern is weaker and already shows breakdown signs, but with SOX and SOXX not yet confirming in sync, treat it as "suspected breakdown." **NVDA** underperformed SOX on the downside today, confirming that core heavyweight names remain more defensive than the small-caps; **MRVL**'s S&P inclusion catalyst was swallowed by market sentiment, and its nearest support sits well below current price; **AVGO** has already tagged the aggressive 370–385 zone, with safer levels still at 324–365.

## 5 Next Week

The S&P's most critical level is around 7232. It isn't just chart support — it's also the CTA sell trigger zone. The index is roughly two points away; a break below could see systematic selling and sentiment-driven pressure compound on each other. The reasonable worst-case pullback zone for June starts at the 6800–7000 area — roughly a 10% retracement from the 7600 high, near the 200-day moving average. Hold that level and this remains a technical correction within an uptrend; lose 6800 and the character shifts to a bear trend.

Nasdaq volatility will be larger; the 620–630 line is the more extreme correction zone, near the 200-day. **TSLA** has broken its uptrend line; if 380 doesn't hold, the next stop is 368 and potentially lower — do not rush to catch the bounce. The message of today's red candle isn't an immediate death sentence for the bull market — it's a reminder that the tape has switched from "close-your-eyes-and-chase" back to "look at the risk lines first." If semis gap down at next week's open, position size needs to be more honest than opinion.
