0 · Market Read

My read on the tape: short-term chase sentiment is still very hot. All four major indices continued higher, with both the S&P and Nasdaq up more than 1% — but this is not an environment where the logic across asset classes has unified. Crude sits above 100, equities keep ripping, and bonds and rates are each trading their own script. That divergence can persist short-term, because the market right now is trading its own expectations and headline noise, not a stable macro framework.

I would not simply read this as a full-on, risk-free trend regime. The Strait of Hormuz, the ceasefire negotiations, and the inconsistency of messaging out of Iran are still whipsawing oil. If elevated crude and supply risk persist long enough, equities can't look the other way forever. Short-term, the longs are still in charge — but I'd define this as a chase-and-earnings-driven phase, not a long-term confirmation you can chase blindly.

1 · Risk Watch

I'm tracking two categories of risk most closely. First is geopolitical and oil risk. The US-Iran negotiating timeline keeps flip-flopping, and there's inconsistency between different factions inside Iran. Hormuz-related events can make the divergence between oil and risk assets snap back into line at any moment. The fact that they look unrelated right now doesn't mean they won't suddenly re-price together.

Second is AI capex and software-valuation risk. TSLA's Q1 free cash flow is briefly positive, but the forward capex bar is higher, and the pressure for FCF to turn negative through the rest of 2026 is already on the table. NOW's print, meanwhile, is a reminder that legacy software companies must prove AI is an incremental tailwind, not a replacement risk. As long as AI revenue comes in slow or margins get revised down, the multiple on the software complex gets re-compressed.

2 · Names to Watch

I'm closely tracking MSFT, IGV, NOW, and TSLA. MSFT has cleared 430 — structurally, it has transitioned from a downtrend into right-side repair, and the more probable path from here is a slow trend of rising lows and rising highs. But its beta isn't huge, the pre-earnings move next week isn't dramatic, and I wouldn't bet a short-term option on a single rip candle. IGV is one step away from a breakout — I've moved the new watch level to 89.1. Whether it reclaims that level determines whether active buyers step in to replace short covering.

NOW's post-close wreck isn't mainly about the quarter's numbers — it's that the forward margin guide came in clearly below expectations, and the upward revision to AI revenue wasn't enough to dispel the medium-term disruption fear. TSLA — I still view the current zone only as a swing-trading environment, the 315-to-465 range thesis is unchanged, and I'd only deploy long-duration positioning at lower levels. AMD and AMZN have either made or are pushing new highs; I'll keep following the trend, but the closer AMD gets to 350 the more appropriate it becomes to trim sector concentration. GOOG — after clearing 325, the target is still the prior high. NVDA — slow-grind bull regime intact.

3 · Trading Strategy

My strategy is to acknowledge the short-term strength but not chase the most crowded spots. MSFT — if it pulls back into the 410–421 zone, I'd add 4% and not wait for an elaborate bottoming pattern, because the real support zone is 397–430 and a pullback into the middle of it is already actionable. If it rips directly instead, I'll ride the existing position and not rush to add.

IGV — only get more aggressive after a confirmed break above 89.1. Otherwise, keep watching whether support at 86–87-plus holds. TSLA — treat it primarily as swing and option speculation; no long adds in the 300s — below 200 is closer to a long-duration zone. On AMD and AMZN, which have already printed new highs, I'll ride the trend or gradually trim concentration. ISRG — still waiting for a sub-430 opportunity. On overall exposure: I'll stay offensive, but treat geopolitics, oil, AI spend, and earnings follow-through as the tripwires for cutting risk at any time.
