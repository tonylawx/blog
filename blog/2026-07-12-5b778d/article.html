Market Recap: Macro Headwinds and Hawkish Fed Pressure

On Monday, global financial markets faced multiple macro headwinds. Major indices drifted lower, with the tech-heavy Nasdaq 100 dropping 1.88%. The semiconductor sector took the brunt of the sell-off, plummeting nearly 5% with all 30 index components ending the session in the red. The bearish sentiment was triggered by rising geopolitical risks and a sudden hawkish shift in Fed policy expectations. On the geopolitical front, Trump plans to re-establish the blockade on Iran, taking control of the Strait of Hormuz and proposing a 20% security transit fee on cargo ships. Driven by these supply-chain concerns, Brent crude surged 9% back above $80 per barrel, heightening global inflation risks. Compounding these worries, Federal Reserve Governor Christopher Waller delivered a hawkish warning, noting that if core CPI continues to print hot, the Fed will consider near-term rate hikes. Interest rate markets have fully priced in a September hike, with a high probability of another hike by March 2027. One of the main physical drivers behind this sticky core inflation is the elevated price of memory chips, which continues to drive up hardware acquisition costs across the tech supply chain.

1 · LIQUIDITY

Leverage Collapse: The Warning from the Korean Stock Market Liquidation

In a high-rate environment with contracting liquidity, over-leveraged speculation is facing a brutal deleveraging process. The South Korean KOSPI index plunged nearly 9% on Monday, breaking below the key 7,000 level and recording its third-largest single-day drop since the 2008 Lehman Brothers collapse. Notably, the Korean stock market has triggered circuit breakers 7 times this year—out of just 13 occurrences in this century—indicating that over half of the system-wide panic circuit breakers occurred in this year alone. The root cause of this crash lies in massive foreign capital outflows combined with local retail investors buying the dip on high margin debt. Foreign funds have dumped $100 billion over the past few months, leaving Korean retail traders as the sole buyers. These traders borrowed aggressively to purchase falling tech giants like Samsung Electronics (down 10%) and SK Hynix (down 15.4%, off 40% from its highs). As South Korean banks ran out of margin lending capacity on Friday, retail investors were unable to roll over their leverage. When the market sell-off accelerated on Monday, a systemic forced liquidation event was triggered. By the close, over 1.2 million retail accounts had received margin calls. Among them, 320,000 to 360,000 retail accounts were forcibly liquidated, wiping out 30 trillion Korean won in capital. While US semiconductors are experiencing high volatility, they have not yet seen systemic forced liquidations. However, the leverage collapse in Korea serves as a stark warning to macro traders. Long-term investors must review their positions and steer clear of high-leverage plays.

2 · CAPEX

Capex and Debt Overhang: The Looming Doom Loop of AI Infrastructure

On the micro level of the AI industry, warnings about bubble risks are starting to be supported by hard data. The development path is unfolding along a clear line: Debt Issuance → Capital Expenditures (Capex) → Monetization Validation. First, tech giants are issuing debt at a record pace. Year-to-date in 2026, six technology giants, including AMZN, GOOG, NVDA, and META, have issued a record $182 billion in investment-grade bonds. This represents 15% of all US corporate bond issuance and over 50% of the growth in total corporate debt. While raising cash through bond markets provides liquidity for massive AI capex, it heavily ties their credit risk to the monetization speed of AI technologies. Second, Free Cash Flow (FCF) will face a severe bottoming test in 2026. Consensus expectations indicate that FCF for the major hyperscalers will bottom out in 2026 before recovering in 2027 and accelerating in 2028. This makes 2026 the ultimate validation year for AI monetization. A key risk is that META and xAI are already starting a price war by offering discounted AI services. If this price war worsens, it will delay revenue growth, shattering expectations of a cash flow recovery in 2027. If AI monetization falls short, massive capex and depreciation expenses will drag down profit margins, leading to lower cash flow forecasts. This credit deterioration could trigger rating downgrades, forcing tech companies to refinance their debt at higher costs, leading to a valuation collapse across the chip and big tech sectors. Given that corporations and governments are far more indebted today than during the 2000 dot-com era, the fallout could be even more severe.

3 · CAPEX

Capex Reversal Risk and Key Semiconductors Defense Lines

To prevent a slide into credit collapse, tech giants may choose to cut AI capex aggressively in 2027 if monetization remains weak. If these companies cut capex to negative growth next year, the current optimistic earnings estimates for the semiconductor and server supply chains will face a sharp downward revision. The key indicator for this risk will be the capex guidance for 2027 in the upcoming big tech earnings releases, making the next earnings season a critical event for investors. On the technical front, the semiconductor sector is trading in a defensive consolidation range. SOXX (Philadelphia Semiconductor Index) has repeatedly failed to retake its uptrend, facing rejection at the 621 resistance level and consolidating below it. SMH (Semiconductor ETF) is showing relative strength, trading around 585 with its key support line sitting at 557. Overall, this major pullback is a process of forced deleveraging and rotation out of overvalued names, rather than the end of the industry's secular growth. For example, after this deep correction, NVDA's forward valuation has dropped to its lowest level since 2019. With the company maintaining a dominant 80% market share in AI silicon, this transition is simply NVDA shifting from a high-growth momentum stock to a high-moat value stock. In this volatile environment, investors should avoid leverage traps, monitor upcoming capex guidance closely, and wait for the market to find its footing.
