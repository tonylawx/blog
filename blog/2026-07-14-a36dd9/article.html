Macro Friction: Geopolitics and Rate Hikes Squeeze Markets

Geopolitical friction and tight monetary policy are putting major weight on tech and semiconductor sectors. Tensions flared as Trump announced plans to restore the Iran blockade and control the Strait of Hormuz, proposing a 20% security fee on transit vessels. Brent crude jumped 9% to top $80, fueling inflation fears and directly hitting risk assets. Meanwhile, Fed Governor Waller issued a hawkish warning: if CPI data comes in hot this week, near-term rate hikes will be on the table. Swaps markets reacted quickly, pricing in a 70%+ chance of a September rate hike, with a second hike priced for March 2027. This rapid tightening puts significant valuation pressure on highly leveraged tech names and rate-sensitive AI infrastructure stocks.

1 · SEMIS

Margin Liquidation: Korean Market Crash Warns Semiconductor Speculators

High interest rates and macro friction triggered a painful deleveraging event. The KOSPI crashed nearly 9%, falling below the 7000 level to post its third-largest single-day drop since the 2008 Lehman collapse. The Korean market has triggered circuit breakers seven times this year, signaling severe liquidity panic. Foreign funds have fled, selling $100 billion over recent months. In contrast, local retail investors borrowed heavily to buy the dip in Samsung and SK Hynix. Memory chipmaker SK Hynix fell 15.4% in a single day. Over the weekend, banks cut margin lending limits. As credit dried up, a massive liquidation panic hit on Monday: 320,000 to 360,000 retail margin accounts were forcibly liquidated, wiping out retail balances to zero. This leverage unwind serves as a warning to retail traders using high leverage in US semiconductor names.

2 · RISK

AI Debt Line: FCF Valuation Pressure on Issuing Giants

Micro risks are building as massive capital expenditures face slow AI monetization. Tech giants including AMZN, GOOG, NVDA, META, ORCL, and SpaceX have issued a record $182 billion in investment-grade debt this year—up 13% from last year, accounting for 15% of all US corporate bond issuance. Debt is funding the current AI buildup. If these multi-billion dollar capital expenditures do not translate into rapid revenue growth and free cash flow (FCF), giants will have to borrow new debt to pay off the old. This cycle increases debt levels and risks credit downgrades, leading to a "borrowing-higher interest rates-squeezed profit" trap that would trigger a broad market valuation reset.

3 · CAPEX

The 2026 Verification Year: Risk of a CapEx U-Turn in 2027

Consensus estimates put 2026 as the FCF trough for the top four tech giants, with a strong recovery expected in 2027. However, aggressive pricing from META and xAI is starting to slow down industry revenue growth. If AI monetization fails to show returns in 2026, the subsequent bubble burst could exceed the scale of the 2000 dot-com crash. To protect their balance sheets, tech giants may execute a hard U-turn in 2027, cutting CapEx budgets to negative growth. Such a cut would shred existing semiconductor earnings forecasts and shake the entire hardware supply chain.

4 · SEMIS

Semiconductor Strategy: Nvidia's Valuation Floor and Deleveraging

Unlike the retail-driven panic in Korea, recent volatility in US chip names (via SOXX and SMH) reflects institutional deleveraging and position rotations rather than systemic forced sales. Removing leverage now makes the medium-term path healthier. Long-term investors do not need to turn overly bearish. Stronger names with deep moats offer solid defense. For instance, NVDA's forward valuation (PE) has fallen to its lowest level since 2019. Even if its dominant 80% market share leads to slower growth ahead, the stock is transitioning from a high-growth play into a stable value anchor, making it a target for patient capital during dips.
